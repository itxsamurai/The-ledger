What's actually allowed, by region

Legal status depends on species, country, and often the sub-national level below that. Filter the ledger by region to see how the same kind of animal can be treated completely differently a few borders over.

Care basics, honestly stated

Legal to own isn't the same as easy to keep, and this part barely changes by region. Each line is a commitment category — the dots show relative demand compared to a typical cat or dog, not an exact score.

Enclosure & environment

Purpose-built housing with the right temperature gradient, humidity, UVB, or flight space — often the single biggest upfront cost.

Diet & feeding

Many exotics need live or specialty food, precise supplementation, or feeding schedules that don't match a human routine.

Section 03

Getting permitted, step by step

The order below is the sequence most permit processes follow globally, even though the agencies and timelines differ. Skipping ahead — acquiring the animal before the paperwork — is the most common way people end up out of compliance anywhere in the world.

1

Confirm species status at every level

Check international (CITES), national, and state/provincial or local rules — a "yes" at one level doesn't override a "no" at another.

Typical time: 1–3 days of research
2

Contact the issuing authority

Usually a national environment ministry or wildlife department. Ask directly which permit class your species falls under and what it costs.

Typical time: 1–3 weeks for a response
3

Prepare facility documentation

Enclosure diagrams, security measures, and sometimes a vet-of-record letter are required before an application is even reviewed.

Typical time: 2–4 weeks
4

Application & inspection

Submit paperwork and fees, then schedule a facility inspection. Many jurisdictions require this before the animal arrives, not after.

Typical time: 4–8 weeks
5

Renewal & recordkeeping

Most permits expire annually and require updated inspections. Keep acquisition records — you'll likely need them for renewal, transport, or rehoming.

Ongoing
Asia

Enforcement and permitting infrastructure vary sharply between countries — some have detailed licensing, others rely mostly on import/export control.

Africa

Many rules center on protecting native species from export; domestic private-keeping regulation is less standardized country to country.

Europe

The EU sets a shared baseline (CITES, invasive-species lists) but individual states layer on their own positive or banned-species lists.

Americas

In the US and Canada, most authority sits at the state/provincial level, so requirements can change completely when you cross a state line.

Oceania & Other

Australia and New Zealand have some of the strictest biosecurity and export controls anywhere, prioritizing native ecosystem protection.
